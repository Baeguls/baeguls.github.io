No Workers in the Workers' Party

On one particularly cold Sunday in Crown Heights, one hundred of Brooklyn’s hippest have assembled together in a ken- do club to discuss some of the most pressing matters of our time. The crowd, seated between a powwow drum and a hand-paint- ed banner that reads PEOPLE OVER PROFIT, skews young and white. They stir when a young woman rises to address them: “Comrades” — a pause — “we finally bought a podium!” Today’s points of discussion at the Party for Socialism and Liberation include practical tips to prepare for deportation (buy a storage unit) and the federally mandated shutdown of a gay social club at Google. There is no doubt in my mind that many of my fellow comrades arrived here today in Ubers. As one boy takes to the stand, the PEOPLE OVER PROFIT sign falls to the ground because it was hung with scotch tape. Is this the “radical left” that keeps conservatives up at night? There is a single, hopeful moment: a young tech worker ad- dresses the crowd, seeking advice from unionized workers. Team- sters and District Council men snap to attention as he announces his mission to wrestle some power away from his billionaire CEO. He wants to start a union. The Party for Socialism and Liberation has passion (often misdirected), aspiring politicians (kids with good hearts), and a brand new podium. It may be the party for you.
